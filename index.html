Bengal Cat

Known for their striking spotted coats and energetic personalities, Bengal cats are a blend of domestic and wild feline traits. The Bengal cat is a hybrid breed originating in the United States, created by crossing the Asian leopard cat (Prionailurus bengalensis) with domestic cats, such as the Egyptian Mau and Abyssinian, to develop a distinct feline. Appearance-wise, Bengals feature coats with spots, rosettes, arrowhead markings, or marbling, making them the only domestic breed with rosette patterns; they also have longer rear legs and carry their tails low.

In personality, they are bred to exhibit a friendly demeanor, often requiring crosses with sociable domestic breeds to ensure kittens manifest this trait, as wildcat ancestry may otherwise influence behavior. Rare aspects include their hybrid status, with early generations (F1-F3) considered foundation stock and sometimes infertile in males; some Bengals carry a recessive long-hair gene, resulting in semi-long-haired variants known as Cashmere Bengals, which are gaining recognition in registries like TICA since 2017.

Personality Traits

  • Intelligent and curious
  • Affectionate and playful
  • Energetic and active
  • Dog-like loyalty
  • Needs regular mental and physical stimulation

Grooming Routines

  • Weekly brushing for coat shine.

  • Regular nail trimming
  • Ear and eye cleaning
  • Oral hygiene maintenance

Feeding Guidelines

  • High-protein raw or wet diet preferred.
  • Adults: 3-5% body weight daily.
  • Kitten: Up to 10% body weight daily.
  • Avoid dry food if possible.
  • Supplement with vitamins and minerals as needed.

Care Tips

Health Risk

  • PKD: Kidney cysts.
  • IBD/FLUTD: Digestive/urinary issues.

Savannah Cat

The Savannah cat is a striking hybrid breed known for its tall, slender build and spotted coat, resembling a wild serval. Originating from a cross between a domestic cat and a serval, Savannahs are known for their intelligence, curiosity, and energetic nature.

The Savannah cat is a hybrid breed originating in the United States from crossing a serval (Leptailurus serval) with a domestic cat (Felis catus), first produced in 1986 by Judee Frank. Appearance-wise, it features a tall, lean build with large erect ears, long legs, a long slender neck, small head, and spotted coat in patterns like brown-spotted tabby or silver-spotted tabby, often with ocelli on ears, tear-streak markings, and a short ringed tail. Personality includes loyalty, following owners, leash training, fetching, and affinity for water play, though early generations may show territorial behaviors, aggression, or litter box issues. Rare aspects involve its hybrid nature, with F1‚ÄìF2 males sterile due to genetic incompatibilities, high serval percentages in backcrosses (up to 87.5%), and legal restrictions in places like Australia and New Zealand due to potential wildlife threats.

Chausie Cat

The Chausie is a domestic cat breed developed by crossing the jungle cat (Felis chaus), native to Southeast Asia, India, the Middle East, and North Africa's Nile Delta, with domestic cats (Felis catus). Hybrids likely originated in ancient Egypt, where both species coexisted, with modern breeding starting in the late 1960s and formalized in the 1990s by dedicated breeders. Recognized by The International Cat Association (TICA) in 1995 as a non-domestic hybrid source breed, it achieved championship status in 2013. Appearance-wise, Chausies are medium to large, with males weighing 11-16 pounds (5.0-7.3 kg) and females 8-13 pounds (3.6-5.9 kg). They have long, leggy bodies, deep chests, broad ears set high, prominent cheekbones, and flattened half-oval eyes. Coat colors are limited to solid black, black grizzled tabby, and black ticked tabby, with gold or yellow eye color preferred. The grizzled and ticked patterns, unique to Chausies due to jungle cat ancestry, develop over time, often starting black in kittens and showing banded or ticked hairs by adulthood. In personality, Chausies are intelligent, active, and athletic, bred from outgoing breeds like the Abyssinian and Oriental Shorthair. They are playful, curious, and form deep bonds with people, disliking being alone and getting along with dogs if raised together. However, they may be prone to food allergies due to shorter intestinal tracts from non-domestic ancestors, requiring high-quality, low-plant-ingredient diets. Rare aspects include its hybrid origins, making it one of few domestic breeds with wild cat lineage, and unique black-on-black tabby patterns not found in purely domestic cats.

Personality Traits

  • Intelligent and active.
  • Playful and curious.
  • Forms deep bonds with people.
  • Dislikes being alone.
  • Even-tempered with families.

Grooming Routines

  • Weekly soft brushing to maintain coat health.
  • Regular nail trims to prevent overgrowth.
  • Avoid over-brushing to prevent skin irritation.
  • Check ears regularly for cleanliness.
  • Dental care with regular brushing.
  • Bathing as needed, but not too frequently.

Feeding Guidelines

  • High-meat, plant-free diet.
  • Avoid grains / veggies due to IBS risk.
  • Raw diet with taurine supplementation.
  • Up to 77% meat content food.
  • Balanced raw with eggs post-2 years.

Care Tips

  • IBD: Plant food sensitivity.
  • Food allergies: Grains / gluten/ veggies. Monitor diet closely.
  • Hip dysplasia: Joint pains, monitor for signs of discomfort.
  • Parasites: Infection risks.
  • Malnutrition: Ensure balanced diet.

Toyger Cat

The Toyger is a domestic cat breed originating in the United States in the 1980s, developed by Judy Sugden by breeding domestic short-haired tabbies to resemble a "toy tiger," with the goal of inspiring tiger conservation. It was recognized for registration by The International Cat Association in the early 2000s and achieved full championship status in 2012. Appearance-wise, Toygers have a muscular, athletic build with a medium to deep torso, long legs, and a very long tail. They feature a short, dense coat with bold, vertically aligned mackerel tabby stripes in dark markings over a bright pumpkin ground color, a circular facial pattern with an "M" on the forehead, and small, round ears with desirable thumb marks. Rare aspects include its use of foundation stock from various countries, such as Bengals and imported cats from India for specific traits like ear markings, and its high cost, with kittens up to $5,000, alongside limited breeders (30 active as of 2024 and 469 registered in 2020).

Personality Traits

  • Sociable and friendly.
  • Intelligent and curious.
  • Playful and energetic.
  • Affectionate with family members.
  • Good with children and other pets.
  • Adaptable to various living environments.

Grooming Routines

  • Weekly brushing for loose fur.
  • Rubber glove for coat maintenance.
  • Bathing if needed, occasional.
  • Regular nail trims.
  • Ear cleaning routinely.

Feeding Guidelines

  • Grain-free, 30% + protein diet.
  • Supplement with raw chicken or turkey.
  • Two small meals daily.
  • 80% raw meat, 10% bone, 10% organ.
  • Consult vet for tailored needs.

Care Tips

  • Weekly brushing for shedding.
  • Meat-rich balanced diet.
  • Nail trims; ear/dental care.
  • Toys/scratching posts/playtime.
  • Routine vet/vaccines/parasites.

Health Risks

  • HCM: Heart thickening.
  • PRA: Blindness.
  • PKD: Red blood cells.
  • Heart murmurs: Underlying issues.
  • FLUTD: Urinary problems.

Serengeti Cat

The Serengeti cat, a hybrid breed originating in the United States, was developed in 1994 by biologist Karen Sausman through crossing a Bengal cat and an Oriental Shorthair. It aims to resemble a serval without recent wild cat blood. Appearance includes a spotted coat (tabby, ebony silver, ebony smoke, or solid black), long legs, large round-tipped ears, and a long neck. Males weigh 10-15 lb (4.5-6.8 kg), females 8-12 lb (3.6-5.4 kg). Rare aspects: recognized by TICA, healthy generally with minor urinary crystal issues; spots vary by pattern, with ghost spotting on solid black.

Personality Traits

  • Energetic and playful.
  • Affectionate and gentle
  • Confident and outgoing
  • Curious and brave
  • Loyal and chatty

Grooming Routines

  • Weekly brushing for coat health.
  • Soft-bristled brush or mitt.
  • Nail trimming regularly.
  • Ear cleaning inspections.
  • Dental care maintenance.

Feeding Guidelines

  • High-protein, meat-based diet.
  • Avoid non-meat foods like veggies/dairy.
  • Carnivore-specific: 8+ lbs chicken/fish per 16.5 lbs food.
  • Balanced commercial or raw.
  • Consult vet for portions.

Care Tips

  • Weekly brushing for coat.
  • Nail trims; ear/dental hygiene.
  • Space/toys for play.
  • Minimal baths/grooming.
  • Monitor urinary; balanced diet.

Health Risks

  • Cardiomyopathy: Heart conditions.
  • Pancreatitis: Pancreas inflammation.
  • Eye issues: Vision problems.
  • Bladder stones: Urinary tract.
  • Immune diseases: Infections.

Egyptian Mau Cat

The Egyptian Mau is a natural breed of domestic cat known for its striking spotted coat and graceful appearance. It is one of the few naturally spotted breeds of domesticated cat, with a history that dates back to ancient Egypt, where depictions of similar cats can be found in artwork and hieroglyphs. Appearance-wise, Egyptian Maus are medium-sized cats with a muscular build, long legs, and a distinctive spotted coat that can come in various colors, including silver, bronze, and smoke. They have large, almond-shaped green eyes and a unique "M" shape on their foreheads. Their spots are randomly distributed across their bodies, giving each cat a unique pattern. In personality, Egyptian Maus are known for their intelligence, agility, and playful nature. They are highly active cats that enjoy interactive play and are known for their ability to jump and climb with ease. They are also affectionate and can form strong bonds with their human companions, often following them around the house. Rare aspects include their natural spotting, which is uncommon among domestic cats, and their history as one of the oldest known cat breeds, with a lineage that can be traced back thousands of years.
